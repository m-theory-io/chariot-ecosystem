   * **No fee for replacing card or cashing out (unless lost/stolen)**
     The issuer may not charge a fee to replace the card or to provide the remaining balance in another form, unless the card was lost or stolen. ([Legal Information Institute][11])
   * **State Law Supremacy / Complement**
     The EFTA / gift card rules do *not* preempt state laws that provide *greater* consumer protection regarding inactivity fees, expiration, etc. Thus, issuers must also check state statutes. ([Consumer Compliance Outlook][19])
   * **Treatment of â€œgeneral-use prepaid cardsâ€**
     Some gift cards bear network logos (Visa, Mastercard) and can be used broadly. These are sometimes considered â€œgeneral-use prepaid cardsâ€ and may have additional overlap with prepaid-account rules (or be subject to hybrid rules). ([America's Credit Unions][20])

3. **State Unclaimed Property / Escheatment / Abandoned Balances**

4. **Operational / Commercial Considerations**

   * Many issuers avoid dormancy fees altogether to simplify compliance and consumer goodwill.
   * Issuers often adopt mechanisms to prevent breakage disputes, maintain reserves, track liabilities, and handle redemption / clawback issues.
   * Some states require special licensing or reporting for gift card issuers in certain circumstances.

5. **Consumer Rights / Limitations**

   * Unlike full prepaid accounts, some of the stronger protections (e.g. error resolution under EFTA, â€œlost card liabilityâ€ limits) may not fully apply to gift cards. ([Federal Register][18])
